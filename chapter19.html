The Midnight Vow
Chapter Nineteen

Thermodynamics of a Kiss

In the dark, you stop being two people. You become one organism—one set of lungs, one beating heart, one singular, lethal intent.

The maintenance shaft was a tomb of rusted pipes and hissing steam, but it was the only sanctuary we had. Above us, through the metal grating, I could hear the Valerian twins pacing the mezzanine, their footsteps heavy and confident, the rhythmic thud-thud of a hunting party that thought they’d already won.

"The boiler release valve is six feet to the left," Lily whispered, her voice barely audible over the screech of the steam. She was pressed so tightly against me that I could feel the erratic rhythm of her heart against my chest. Her hands were still working on the final knot of the tourniquet, her fingers light and precise even in the total darkness.

"If I turn the primary wheel, it’ll vent the entire floor with high-pressure, superheated steam," I murmured, my hand tracing the line of her jawline. The proximity was intoxicating—the scent of her skin, clean and cold, warring with the smell of blood and industrial grease.

Lily paused. Her fingers stilled against my bicep, then she looked up. In the faint red flicker of the distant emergency lights, her eyes were bottomless, searching mine with a hunger that had nothing to do with survival.

"They’ll be vaporized instantly," she breathed.

"They played with ballistics," I replied, my voice dropping to a low, dangerous hum. "Let them learn the cost of heat."

I shifted, my injured arm throbbing, but I didn't care. I leaned in, and for a moment, the war outside, the gunshots, and the slaughter of the night vanished. She didn't wait. She tilted her head, her lips meeting mine with a desperate, bruising force that tasted like copper and iron.

It wasn't a soft, romantic kiss. It was a collision. It was an affirmation of existence in a world that was trying to erase us. Her hands slid up to the back of my neck, tangling in my hair, pulling me into her space until there was no air left between us.

When we broke apart, both of us were breathless, our eyes locked in the dark. The sociopathic coldness that defined us was still there, but it was molten now, burning with a frantic, shared need.

"After this," she whispered, her thumb tracing the corner of my mouth, "we finish the floor. No survivors."

"Agreed," I said.

I reached up, grabbing the massive, grease-slicked iron wheel of the boiler main. I didn't hold back. I shoved with every ounce of remaining strength in my right arm. The metal screamed as it turned, years of rust breaking away in a shower of orange flakes.

CLANG.

The floorboards above us groaned. A massive, deafening roar ripped through the atrium—the sound of superheated steam erupting from the floor grates.

Above, the twins screamed. It wasn't the sound of men who had been shot. It was a primal, gut-wrenching shriek of agony as the pressure wave hit them. The floor beneath their feet didn't just vibrate; it buckled.

The weight of the steam vent’s release was too much. The mezzanine floorboards, weakened by the chandelier’s impact minutes before, snapped. We stayed huddled in the shaft, watching through the grating as the floor section collapsed. The Valerians plummeted into the smoke below, the suppressed pistol skittering across the floor and sliding directly into the grate near my hand.

I reached through the slats and snatched it.

The silence that followed was absolute, save for the rhythmic dripping of water and the distant, dying hiss of the boiler. We waited for a full minute, listening. Nothing.

I looked at Lily. She was already pushing the grating aside, climbing out into the debris-strewn mezzanine. She looked back at me, her hair disheveled, her blouse torn, and a look of absolute, terrifying predatory grace on her face.

"Gun," she said, holding out her hand.

I handed it to her. She checked the chamber—full magazine.

"They’re dead, Bruce," she said, looking down at the wreckage below. "Which means the library, the administration wing, and the main armory are all wide open. We have forty-two hours left."

She stepped back over to me, her hand sliding down my chest, her fingers lingering over the blood-stained bandage on my arm. She leaned in, her voice dropping to a whisper that felt like a caress.

"You're bleeding again," she noted, her eyes dark with a mixture of concern and a dark, twisted pride. "But you're the most lethal thing in this building. Let's go finish the board."

I took her hand. We didn't walk; we stalked out into the red-stained corridor, armed, bleeding, and finally ready to take the school.
