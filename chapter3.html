The Midnight Vow
Chapter Three

The Restricted Section

The day is just a stage, and when the lights go out, the real me finally steps down from it.

Jack was snoring lightly across the room. I slipped out of bed, leaving the 'normal guy' mask resting on my pillow.

I didn't decide to break into the Headmaster's office on a whim. For three weeks, I had been quietly running a background script on the academy's closed network. I had noticed an anomaly in the administration's firewall—a hidden data stream operating on a strict modulo cyclicity, shifting its encryption keys in a predictable, repeating loop every 24 hours. It wasn't just a glitch. It was a digital lockbox.

When I finally broke the sequence tonight, it didn't decrypt a digital file. It spat out a physical directory coordinate: Archive Cabinet B, Headmaster's Private Study.

The hallways of this prison are dead silent at 1 A.M. I know the patrol routes better than the night guards do. It took me less than two minutes to reach the heavy oak door.

People think a locked door means safety. It just means they have something worth hiding.

I pulled out a small piece of tension wire I had pocketed earlier and picked the old brass lock in under ten seconds. The click echoed loudly in the dark, but no footsteps followed.

I slipped inside, keeping my flashlight pointed at the floor. I went straight for the heavy steel archive cabinets in the back. Cabinet B. My file.

I pulled it out, but it was suspiciously thin. There were no academic records, no psychological evaluations from when I was dumped here. There was just a single, faded Polaroid photograph.

I stared at the picture, and for the first time in years, my mask slipped completely. I couldn't breathe.

It was my mom and dad. They looked exactly my age, maybe seventeen or eighteen. But they were standing in this exact office. Surrounding them was a group of other students, and standing right behind my father was a much younger version of our current Headmaster.

That wasn't what made my blood run cold. On my father's jacket, and drawn in black ink on my mother's wrist, was a strange symbol—a crescent moon with a dagger running through it.

They didn't just randomly send me to this school. My parents were a part of whatever dark game was being played here. And that car crash... it wasn't an accident. It was an execution.
