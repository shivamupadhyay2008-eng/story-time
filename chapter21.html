The Midnight Vow
Chapter Twenty-One

The Apex Variable

The greatest mistake you can make when fighting a monster is assuming it plays by human rules. They thought the dark would blind us. They forgot that the dark is where I was built.

The heavy steel door slammed into the floorboards, kicking up a cloud of pulverized drywall. The Server Hub was pitch black, save for the strobing, blinding flashes of white-hot muzzle fire as the Wardens swept the room with suppressed submachine guns.

They moved with the cold, synchronized efficiency of professional killers. Thermal optics, Kevlar vests, tactical spacing. They were looking for a panicked teenager and his terrified girlfriend cowering behind a server rack.

They didn't realize they had just walked into a cage with a dormant weapon.

I didn't cower. The moment the lights died, I had scaled the heavy iron lattice of the primary server bank. I was crouched ten feet in the air, perched on the narrow steel grating like a gargoyle, watching the green laser sights cut through the dark beneath me.

"Sweep the aisles," the lead Warden ordered, his voice muffled by his tactical mask. "Clear and burn."

The Warden on the left stepped into my aisle. He raised his rifle, the flashlight mounted on the barrel cutting a narrow beam through the dust.

I didn't jump; I dropped.

I landed silently on his shoulders, my knees locking around his neck in a brutal, bone-snapping vice. Before he could even pull the trigger, I drove my hunting knife directly down into the unarmored gap between his Kevlar collar and his helmet. The blade sank to the hilt. He collapsed without a sound, the submachine gun clattering against the metal grate.

"Contact!" the second Warden yelled, spinning toward the noise.

He never completed the turn.

From the shadows at the far end of the aisle, two suppressed shots coughed in rapid succession. Pfft. Pfft. Lily didn't aim for center mass where the body armor was thickest. She aimed for the optical lenses of his night-vision goggles.

The Warden went down hard, his rifle discharging wildly into the ceiling as he hit the floor.

The Lead Warden was the only one left. He was a massive, scarred veteran, and he didn't panic. He instantly dropped his rifle, recognizing the close-quarters disadvantage, and drew a serrated combat knife, lunging into the darkness where I stood.

He swung a heavy, lethal arc aimed at my throat.

I didn't block it. I stepped inside the arc, letting the serrated blade slice a shallow line across my collarbone, and drove my forehead violently into the bridge of his nose. The sickening crunch of cartilage echoed in the quiet room.

As he stumbled back, stunned, I swept his legs, sending him crashing into the heavy glass casing of the mainframe. I pinned him there, my forearm crushing his windpipe. He thrashed, his heavy combat boots kicking wildly, trying to dislodge me.

I leaned in, my face inches from his, my eyes dead and devoid of any human resonance.

"You were looking for the variable," I whispered, my voice an icy, metallic rasp. "You found it."

I drove my knee into his ribs, hearing the snap of bone, and twisted my blade upward into his ribcage. He gasped, his eyes going wide with shock, before the light permanently faded from them.

I let his massive body slide to the floor. The Server Hub fell dead silent, save for the low, dying hum of the backup batteries.

I stood in the absolute dark, my chest heaving, the blood of three professional killers coating my hands. The realization from the encrypted files was singing in my veins. I wasn't pretending anymore. I was exactly what my mother designed me to be.

Footsteps approached softly from the shadows.

Lily stepped into the narrow aisle, the suppressed pistol lowered to her side. The faint green LED lights from the backup servers illuminated her face. She looked at the three dead Wardens, then slowly lifted her gaze to me.

She didn't look horrified. She looked completely, utterly captivated.

She stepped over the Lead Warden's body, closing the distance between us until there was no space left. She dropped the pistol onto a server rack and reached out, her bloodstained hands sliding up my chest, grabbing the lapels of my torn jacket.

With a fierce, possessive strength, she shoved me back against the heavy glass of the server casing.

"You're a monster," she breathed, her voice thick with a dark, heavy adrenaline.

"Are you scared, Lilian?" I whispered, my hands gripping her waist, my fingers digging into the soft curve of her hips.

"I've never been more turned on in my life," she answered.

She kissed me, and this time, there was no hesitation, no frantic rush of mere survival. It was deep, claiming, and intoxicatingly dangerous. Her mouth was hot and demanding, her tongue tasting the sweat and iron on my lips. My hands slid up her back, tangling violently in her dark hair, pulling her flush against me. I could feel the heat of her skin through her torn blouse, the frantic, erratic pounding of her heart completely syncing with mine.

She let out a soft, sharp gasp into my mouth as I bit down gently on her lower lip, my hands sliding down to grip her thighs, lifting her slightly against the glass. For a long, suffocating moment, the slaughter around us vanished. There was only the heat, the dark, and the intoxicating gravity of what we were becoming together.

But the real world doesn't wait for monsters to catch their breath.

A sharp burst of static hissed through the quiet room. It was coming from the dead Lead Warden’s tactical radio, clipped to his shoulder.

Lily slowly pulled back, her lips swollen, her eyes dark and heavy with desire. She looked down at the radio, her breathing ragged, before reaching down and pressing the transmit button to listen.

A cold, digitized voice crackled through the speaker.

"Control to Alpha Team. The Server Hub has gone dark. Status report."

We remained perfectly silent.

"Alpha Team, do you copy? Sector 4 is being purged. The Headmaster is abandoning the board. He has initiated the Phoenix Protocol and is moving to the underground extraction point."

Lily slowly looked up at me, a lethal, breathtaking smile spreading across her lips.

The game was changing again. The Headmaster wasn't just observing the culling anymore. He was fleeing. And if he made it out, the Crescent would win.

"He thinks he can leave," I said softly, picking up my hunting knife from the floor and wiping the blade on the dead Warden’s vest.

"He’s about to learn," Lily whispered, picking up her pistol and racking the slide, "that the architects don't let anyone leave the building alive."
