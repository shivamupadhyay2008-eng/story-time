The Midnight Vow
Chapter Eighteen

The Brass Wire

Arrogance is a louder alarm than a gunshot. We thought we had conquered the board because we killed the loudest piece. We forgot that true predators let the scavengers tire themselves out before joining the hunt.

Julian’s blood was still pooling on the marble floor. The heavy chemical foam was finally settling, illuminated by the harsh, strobing flashes of the neon-red emergency lights.

For a terrifying, exhilarating second, we owned the Grand Atrium.

Then, a perfectly straight line of vibrant, emerald-green light cut through the red strobes, painting a glowing dot directly onto the center of Lily’s chest.

My instincts triggered before my brain could process the math. I slammed my shoulder into Lily, tackling her hard against the stone pillar just as a suppressed gunshot whispered through the dark.

The heavy caliber bullet shattered the marble exactly where her heart had been a fraction of a second before, showering us in razor-sharp stone splinters.

"Movement! Second floor balcony!" I hissed, pulling her down behind the base of the pillar.

I risked a glance around the stone. Standing on the second-tier landing, perfectly silhouetted against the flashing red lights, were the Valerian twins. Their mother was the High Council’s primary defense contractor. While Julian was hoarding table legs and kitchen knives, the Valerians had spent the last three years quietly dismantling and smuggling a suppressed tactical pistol into their dorm room, piece by piece.

They didn't just have a gun. They had high ground, and they were utterly cold-blooded.

Another suppressed crack. A bullet ripped through the sleeve of my jacket, grazing my bicep. The sudden, burning sting was a violent reminder of our own mortality. We were smart. We were vicious. But a hunting knife cannot parry copper-jacketed lead.

"We can't hold this!" Lily shouted over the deafening mechanical wail of the alarms. She looked at the blood rapidly soaking through my torn sleeve. For the first time since the lockdown began, true panic flickered in her eyes. "Bruce, you're hit!"

"It's a graze," I lied, the muscles in my left arm seizing with a fiery, sickening heat. "The maintenance hatch behind the library. If we cross the atrium, we can drop into the steam tunnels."

"It's twenty yards of open ground," Lily calculated, her voice tight. "We won't make it."

"We will if they can't see us."

I unclipped the heavy brass flashlight from the dead rower's belt. I didn't turn it on. I locked eyes with Lily.

"On my mark. Run."

I hurled the heavy brass flashlight across the atrium floor. It clattered violently against the far wall. The green laser instantly snapped to the noise, and two suppressed shots shattered the plaster where the flashlight landed.

"Now!"

We broke cover. We didn't run like heirs; we ran like animals. The green laser slashed frantically through the smoke, trying to track us. A bullet clipped the heel of my boot. Another shattered the glass door of the library just as we threw our weight against it, tumbling into the pitch-black archives.

I slammed the heavy iron lock shut, my left arm screaming in agony. Lily didn't hesitate. She grabbed the edge of the heavy iron bookshelf and pulled. I threw my right shoulder into it, and together we toppled three tons of books and steel against the door, barricading it entirely.

We stumbled backward into the darkness, slipping into the narrow, claustrophobic maintenance shaft hidden behind the rear stacks.

I collapsed against the freezing steel pipes, my breathing ragged and heavy. The adrenaline was fading, replaced by a cold, trembling shock. My left arm was completely useless, coated in warm, slick blood that looked black in the dim glow of the ventilation LEDs.

Lily dropped to her knees in front of me in the cramped shaft. There was barely enough room for us to breathe. We were pressed chest-to-chest in the suffocating dark.

"Show me," she demanded, her voice shaking as she ripped the torn fabric of my sleeve away.

The bullet had dug a deep, ugly trench across my bicep. Lily cursed softly, instantly shrugging off her trench coat and tearing a long strip of fabric from her blouse.

"Hold still," she ordered, her hands trembling as she wrapped the makeshift tourniquet tightly around my arm.

"I thought... I thought you didn't panic, Lilian," I gasped, the pain making my vision blur at the edges.

"I don't," she snapped, pulling the fabric painfully tight. But when she looked up at me, the cold, sociopathic mask was completely gone. Her dark eyes were wide, flooded with a terrifying, desperate vulnerability. She pressed her bloodstained hands flat against my chest, feeling my heart hammering against my ribs.

"You don't get to die, Bruce," she whispered fiercely, the space between us vanishing as she leaned in, her forehead resting heavily against my collarbone. "You promised me a war. You don't get to leave me alone in this place."

I looked down at her. We were monsters. We had just slaughtered four people without blinking. But trapped in this freezing, pitch-black shaft, bleeding and outgunned, the only thing that felt real was the desperate grip she had on my shirt.

I lifted my good hand, tangling my fingers into her dark hair, pulling her gaze back up to mine.

"I'm not going anywhere," I breathed, my thumb wiping a streak of dust and blood from her cheek. "They have the gun. They have the high ground. But they don't know the architecture of this building."

Lily’s breathing slowed. The terrifying, beautiful intelligence returned to her eyes, hardening like cut diamond.

"The steam vents," she whispered, her lips ghosting against mine in the dark. "The Valerians are standing directly over the high-pressure boiler mains."

"They wanted to play with ballistics," I said, a cold, ruthless smile pulling at my mouth despite the pain. "Let's show them how to play with thermodynamics."
