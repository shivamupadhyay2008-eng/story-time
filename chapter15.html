The Midnight Vow
Chapter Fifteen

The Steel Trap

The adrenaline of a blood vow eventually wears off, but the paranoia never does. When you invite a spider into your bed, you don't sleep. You just lie awake, waiting to see if it weaves a web or sinks its fangs into your neck.

It was 4:00 AM. The rain was still hammering the campus grounds.

I didn't go back to my dorm. Instead, I scaled the slick, rain-soaked stonework of the East Wing, my fingers gripping the ancient ivy until I reached the third-floor balcony of the Honors Dormitory. Before I could even raise my hand to the glass, the heavy window silently unlatched and swung open.

Lily stood in the dark, her silhouette framed by the dim light of her laptop.

"You favor your left foot when you climb," she whispered, stepping aside to let me in. "You need to fix that. It makes your rhythm predictable."

"I'll keep that in mind," I muttered, shaking the freezing rain from my jacket.

I looked around her room. To anyone else, it was the immaculate, sterile sanctuary of a perfect student. But Lily walked over to her massive antique mirror and pressed a hidden latch beneath the frame. The mirror swung open on hidden hinges, revealing a sprawling corkboard pinned with hundreds of surveillance photos, financial documents, and red string.

She hadn't just been watching Julian. She had been tracking every single heir on campus.

My eyes landed on a photograph of myself, right in the center of the board.

"You've been busy," I said, a dark smirk pulling at my lips.

"I don't like surprises," Lily said, walking over to me. She pulled a small, black medical kit from her desk and gestured for me to sit on the edge of her bed. "And I don't like sloppy work. Give me your hand."

I held out my left hand. The crude bandage I had wrapped around my palm in the chapel was already soaked through.

Lily sat beside me, her knee brushing against mine. The physical proximity was suffocating, electric. She unwrapped the bloody gauze with absolute, clinical precision. She didn't flinch at the sight of the deep cut. Instead, she poured antiseptic over the wound.

The alcohol burned like hellfire, but I didn't make a sound. I just watched her eyes.

"If we execute this plan, Bruce," she whispered, her gaze focused intently on the needle she was threading to stitch my palm. "We cross a line we can never come back from. We aren't just destroying reputations anymore. We will be taking lives."

She pulled the first stitch tight. A sharp jolt of pain shot up my arm, but her cold, soft fingers pressing against my wrist kept my pulse racing for an entirely different reason.

"My soul was damned the day my uncle dropped me off at this school," I said quietly, leaning in until my lips were inches from her ear. "What about yours, Lilian?"

Lily looked up, her dark eyes locking onto mine, burning with that same beautiful, apocalyptic hatred.

"I sold mine the day I realized my father was willing to watch me bleed," she breathed.

She tied off the final stitch and bit the excess thread, her lips grazing the edge of my palm. The tension between us was a physical weight, heavy and razor-sharp.

But before I could close the remaining distance between us, the lights in her room suddenly died.

The sudden darkness was accompanied by a heavy, mechanical THUD that shook the floorboards.

Lily leaped up, rushing to her window. "The storm must have knocked out the grid—"

"No," I said, my blood running cold as I looked at my phone. "No signal. They’re jamming us."

Outside the window, heavy, interlocking plates of solid steel descended from the roofline, slamming down over the glass and sealing the balcony shut with a deafening crash. The magnetic deadbolts engaged with a sharp click.

I rushed to the bedroom door, twisting the handle. Locked. The digital keypad on the wall had gone completely dead.

We were trapped.

A small, hidden speaker above Lily's desk crackled to life with a hiss of static. The Headmaster's voice echoed into the room, cold, quiet, and intimately terrifying.

"Good morning to the one percent," the Headmaster said smoothly. "As you know, the rest of the student body was sent home yesterday for the Founders' Week holiday. The campus is entirely empty, save for those of you residing in the Honors wing. You were told you stayed behind for a Leadership Summit."

Lily stared at the speaker, her face turning perfectly pale. The trap hadn't been set for the whole school. It had been set exclusively for the heirs, disguised as a prestigious reward.

"The timeline has been accelerated," the Headmaster's voice continued. "The High Council requires five successors. There are currently forty of you locked inside this wing. You have forty-eight hours to finalize your negotiations. The doors will unlock when thirty-five heartbeats have stopped. Let the Initiation begin."

The intercom clicked off.

The culling wasn't a secret ceremony. It was a brutal, contained reality. And they had just locked a building full of heavily armed, terrifyingly intelligent sociopaths in the dark.

I slowly turned back to Lily. The silver ring on my right hand gleamed in the faint, red glow of the emergency lighting.

The game hadn't just changed. The board had just been locked.
